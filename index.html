How it works

Industrial Engineering

An 11-hour pipeline from raw industrial exhaust to clean, commercially sellable fuel.

Step 01 · 0–4 Hours

Source Capture & Filtration

Factory exhaust passes through nanoparticle air filters — which have excellent adsorptive properties for capturing airborne contaminants across a broad spectrum — then through a large-scale catalytic converter, converting harmful pollutants (CO, hydrocarbons, nitrogen oxides) into CO₂, nitrogen, and water vapour.

What is a Catalytic Converter?

An emissions control device that converts harmful pollutants into less harmful byproducts. Our system uses a significantly upscaled industrial model to process entire factory exhaust streams, reducing three key gases: Carbon Monoxide, Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs), and Nitrogen Oxides.

The clean CO₂ stream then passes through Polystyrene Resin — a modified polymer with ion-exchange and nanoporous materials that selectively absorbs CO₂. Nitrogen and water vapour are safely released back into the atmosphere.

How Polystyrene Resin Works

The resin contains carbonate ions that bind CO₂ when the surrounding environment is dry, and releases it when wet — so CO₂ is extracted simply by immersing the slab in water, forming carbonic acid (H₂CO₃). The modular core technology increases the system's adaptability in both urban and industrial environments.

Step 02 · 4–7 Hours

Electrolysis & Piezoelectric Power

Carbonic acid is introduced into an electrolysis chamber, converting CO₂ into hydrogen gas. Electricity for this process is generated by piezoelectric crystals beneath the factory floor — factory personnel movement induces mechanical stress, generating electricity. Surplus energy is transmitted to the city grid, reducing dependence on external power sources.

Hydrogen gas is captured and stored in graphene-based containers — chosen for their superior surface area-to-volume ratio, thermal and mechanical properties, and ability to shorten hydrogen diffusion distances for faster exchange and reduced release temperature.

Step 03 · 7–11 Hours

Liquefaction & Green Methanol Synthesis

70% of hydrogen is allocated for Green Methanol conversion. The remaining 30% is directed to fuel cells for clean electricity generation with minimal environmental impact.

For methanol conversion, hydrogen undergoes cryogenic liquefaction — cooled below −253°C (−423°F) using helium as a refrigerant in a dedicated cryogenic refrigerator. Liquid CO₂ (sourced from urban polystyrene resin collection) is then injected into the liquid hydrogen stream through Carbon Nanotubes.

Why Carbon Nanotubes for Transport?

CNTs possess impressive chemical stability, large surface area, and hollowness — drawing liquid inside by capillarity. They enable high energy density hydrogen transport with large capacity and transition flexibility at room temperature, ensuring safety at scale. Catalyst materials including Platinum, Rhodium, Palladium, and Al₂O₃ accelerate the conversion reactions.

The mixture passes through a pressure-reducing regulator, a flow controller, and an Al₂O₃ (aluminium oxide) catalyst to accelerate the methanol synthesis reaction. Final Green Methanol is distributed via underground pipelines.

Technical Q&A
Green vs. conventional methanol?

Green methanol is produced using renewable sources (biomass gasification, renewable electricity, captured CO₂). Traditional methanol is derived from fossil fuels. Green methanol reduces CO₂ emissions by up to 95%.

Why sodium perchlorate as the organic electrolyte?

It facilitates easier dissociation of sodium metal, enhancing electrolysis efficiency. The Na anode is kept in organic electrolyte separated by a NASICON membrane to prevent aqueous corrosion.

Why platinum as the cathode?

Platinum is highly resistant to oxidation and remains inert during redox reactions — ensuring unchanged performance across millions of cycles at industrial scale without degradation.

Graphene vs. carbon nanotubes — when to use which?

Graphene is ideal for gaseous hydrogen storage due to its surface properties. Carbon Nanotubes suit liquid hydrogen transport due to their hollow tubular structure and capillary action.

How does the AI monitoring system work?

AI-enhanced monitoring systems track CO₂ levels, emissions, and operational efficiency across all units in real time, coordinated across manufacturing plants and centralised facilities — providing data-driven insights to optimise process efficiency.

Why helium as the cryogenic refrigerant?

Helium efficiently cools hydrogen below −253°C to achieve liquid form, a necessary step in converting hydrogen into green methanol. Regular helium replenishment ensures the cooling process runs continuously at scale.

Active Research Challenges

Polystyrene Resin Challenge

It has been established that standard polystyrene does not possess the capability to absorb CO₂ — a conclusion that is honest and scientifically valid. The development of a modified variant that can effectively capture CO₂ will necessitate extensive research in materials science. We are currently investigating validated CO₂ capture materials, including metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) and amine-based sorbents, as potential alternatives.

Energy Balance Considerations

A significant challenge in the conversion of CO₂ is that the process typically requires more energy input than the energy output generated. While the concept of utilising piezoelectric generation from foot traffic is inventive, it is likely to produce minimal power — insufficient for industrial-scale electrolysis. We are exploring the integration of renewable energy sources, such as solar and wind power, to enhance the energy supply for our system.

Chemical Process Optimisation

The conversion pathway from CO₂ to methanol necessitates more precise reaction conditions. Industrial methanol synthesis conventionally requires elevated temperatures (200–300 °C), high pressures (50–100 atm), and specific catalysts — particularly copper-based systems. We are conducting research on these established processes to reinforce the technical foundation of our approach.

Thermodynamic Efficiency Assessment

The liquefaction of hydrogen demands substantial energy inputs owing to its exceptionally low boiling point. We are evaluating whether maintaining hydrogen in a gaseous state would be more energy-efficient for the entirety of our process, and are reviewing established industrial practices to determine the optimal approach.
